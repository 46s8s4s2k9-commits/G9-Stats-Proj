01 — Introduction

"Does the way you learn shape the grade you earn?"

Population Mean Observational Study Association n = 8,000

This dataset, sourced from Kaggle's Student Lifestyle vs. Academic Performance collection, captures behavioral, socioeconomic, and academic data from 8,000 students. Variables include how students study (Offline, Online, or Hybrid), their family income level (High, Middle, or Low), and their final exam score — an overall measure of academic achievement.

Research Question

Does a student's study method or family income level have a statistically significant association with their final exam score?

Because Final Score is a numerical measure, our question asks about a population mean — the average score across all students in each group. Understanding this question matters for equity in education: if certain study approaches are strongly linked to academic success, that's information students and educators can act on.

02 — Study Design

How Was This Data Collected?

Understanding the structure of the study tells us what conclusions we can and can't draw.

Type of Study

Observational Study

We observe pre-existing characteristics — study method and family income — and their relationship to final scores. No random assignment was made. Students were not randomly placed into study groups; they chose their own methods. This means we can identify associations, but we cannot claim one variable causes changes in the other.

Generalizability

The 8,000 students in this dataset represent a large sample. Our findings describe patterns within this sample. Generalizing to all students worldwide requires caution — the sample's population of origin and selection method affect how broadly these results apply.

04 — Group Comparisons

How Do the Groups Compare?

Mean Final Score broken down by Study Method and Family Income Level.

Average Final Score by Study Method

Hybrid students averaged the highest final score (83.82), followed by Offline (83.02) and Online (82.85). While the overall differences are modest in absolute terms, statistical testing reveals this gap is meaningful. We observed a clear association between using a Hybrid study approach and higher academic performance.

05 — Inferential Statistics

Ruling Out Chance

Could the differences we observed just be random variation? We ran 1,000 simulations to find out.

1
🔍
Observe
Calculate the real mean difference between groups in our data.
2
🔀
Simulate
Randomly shuffle group labels 1,000 times. Compute the mean difference each time — assuming no true effect.
3
📊
Compare
Plot all 1,000 simulated differences. How often did random chance produce a gap as large as ours?
4
✅
Conclude
If p < 0.05, the observed difference is unlikely to be due to chance — we call it statistically significant.
How to read these charts: The bars show the distribution of 1,000 simulated mean differences when there is no real effect. The red vertical line marks the actual observed difference. Red bars are simulations that produced a difference at least as extreme as ours — those are counted to calculate the p-value.

06 — Conclusion

Hybrid learning is associated with higher scores.

Our randomization analysis found statistically significant evidence that students using a Hybrid study method scored higher on average than both Offline and Online learners (p = 0.020 and p = 0.011, respectively).

Family income level showed no statistically significant association with final score at any comparison — the differences were all smaller than 0.26 points and consistent with random variation.

⚠️

Important: Because this is an observational study, we can only conclude that there is a statistically significant association between study method and final score — not that Hybrid studying causes higher scores. Other factors (motivation, discipline, access to resources) may explain the difference.

"The Hybrid approach is the only study method associated with a statistically distinguishable advantage — and income level appears to have no significant bearing on final scores in this sample."

07 — Limitations & Reflection

What Can't We Claim?

Intellectual honesty means acknowledging what our study design can and cannot tell us.

01

No Causal Claims

Because we did not randomly assign students to study methods, we cannot say that Hybrid studying causes higher scores. Students who choose Hybrid methods may already differ in important ways — they may be more motivated, have better resources, or have higher prior GPA. These are confounding variables that could explain the score difference independently of study method.

02

Confounding Variables

Variables not in this dataset — total hours studied per week, prior academic performance, teacher quality, parental involvement, school resources, and motivation level — may all be correlated with both study method and final score. Without controlling for these, we cannot isolate the true effect of study method alone.

03

Generalizability

This dataset comes from a single source. The students' school system, geographic region, and demographic characteristics are not fully described, which limits how broadly we can apply these findings. Results may not generalize to all educational contexts, countries, or grade levels.

If We Could Redo This Study…

To establish stronger evidence and allow for causal conclusions, future research could:

  • Randomly assign students to Offline, Online, or Hybrid groups for a semester — this would allow us to make causal claims about the impact of study method.
  • Collect additional variables such as study hours per week, prior GPA, attendance rate, and parental education level to control for confounders in a regression model.
  • Use a larger, more diverse sample drawn from multiple schools and regions to improve generalizability and external validity.
  • Track students longitudinally across multiple semesters to assess whether the Hybrid advantage is consistent over time.
